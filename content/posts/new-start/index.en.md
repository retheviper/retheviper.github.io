I have only recently become a software engineer, so I still do not understand many things, not only industry trends and popular languages but even programming fundamentals. Even so, I keep learning on the job, and I do not think I can simply keep all of that knowledge in my head. New languages, frameworks, and libraries appear every day, and I still have not properly learned the technologies I have already used. So I decided to start a blog and collect the things I have touched in one place so I can refer back to them later.

I once read that the history of human civilization is a history of records. Once the total amount of human knowledge became too large for a single brain to hold, people invented external storage for information. Thanks to that, we can access knowledge inherited across generations, and even if we do not know something from the start, we can still adapt as needed. For example, after mobile phones became common, nobody needed to memorize phone numbers anymore, but the number of people we could contact actually increased. In that sense, we now live in an era where the ability to find the right knowledge for the right situation matters more than how much knowledge a single person can hold.

I think the programming world is the same. `bash`[^1] is still a powerful tool and can be more convenient than a GUI in some situations. But memorizing every command and typing every path is not efficient. What matters more is whether you can enter the right command when you need it. That is why scripts that collect commands exist, and nowadays I think it is better if we can simply copy and paste what we need at the moment.

When I write about what I have learned, I want this blog to be useful in the same way. For example, if I want to copy a folder from server A to server B, I do not want to just write down `scp` or `rsync`. I want to leave behind a sequence of knowledge that also compares SSH authentication and the differences between SCP and RSYNC. I want this blog to be the kind of place where that information can be found.

[^1]: This includes not only Linux, but also macOS Terminal and Windows PowerShell.
